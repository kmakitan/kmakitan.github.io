Fammili

Fammili is an innovative product that changes how parents plan their weekends with their family. It was designed to help users take a new approach to casual activities. From its look to its experience, Fammili was carefully designed to be human, relatable, and inspiring.

BACKGROUND

Problem Statement

It’s simply impossible to do something extravagant and special every weekend with your family. It requires a ton of coordination, preparation and energy to execute. We had to create a service that provided fun and interesting activities that could be done almost any weekend, while reducing the effort it took to plan and do it.

Visionary Requirements

Innovate

In order to be truly innovative, you have to create something new. You can’t just make something that exists better.

Get it done

Our app had to be designed so that it was difficult for users to back out of their plans, in order to get them out of their repetitive cycles.

Forced Decisions

When selecting an activity for the weekend, users were given only two activity options to choose from, causing them to feel severely limited. When users did not choose an activity, their weekend was automatically filled in. This made users feel less responsible to do the activity because it was not their decision.

Following the user interview & testing sessions, we synthesized our insights and were able to create personas and archetypes. These artifacts created a foundation for product direction.

UX/UI DESIGN

Reference-Based Design Process

While creating the UX and UI of the application, I refined my reference-based design process to the following:
1. Gather many references for a certain aspect of design and group the references into patterns
2. Sketch each pattern at low-fidelity using the product as context
3. Assess and discuss the best patterns for the product
4. Create medium to high fidelity mockups of selected patterns
5. Select the best pattern as a team and validate with users

From the home to the selection screen, this reference-based process was used to design and communicate decisions for all parts of the new activity selection experience.

Visual Design

In order to make the planning process feel stress-free, I designed playful and simple visual elements. Much of my design inspiration also came from Material Design and Flat 2.0.

Late planning (alternative flow)

When deciding on the day of, seeing all possible activities can be overwhelming, even leading to indecision. To prevent this, we filter down their options after they select a mood.

No plan (exception flow)

Not choosing an activity can make parents feel guilty and lazy. Instead, we helped them feel accomplished by creating a No Plan experience, which fills in their weekend with an “Anything Goes” card.

LEARNINGS

Design is Collaborative

When I first joined, being the only designer on the team, I felt as if I had to design the entire application experience myself. I quickly learned that it was the exact opposite, and my job was to connect everyone together to achieve the solutions. I learned how to lead effective brainstorming sessions, communicate design decisions, and work through handoffs with engineers.

Targeted Design

When I first started conducting interviews, I wanted to design to please everyone. This a terrible idea, especially for startups. Startups should always aim to design for the early adopter. Designing for everyone means pleasing no one, so it was important for us narrow down and find our target user. Once we found our target user, the product direction became much clearer.
